# CBT-Style Reframing Basics

Cognitive reframing is a core idea from Cognitive Behavioural Therapy. The basic principle is that the way we interpret a situation affects how we feel about it. By examining and gently adjusting unhelpful thought patterns, we can sometimes ease emotional distress.

## Common Unhelpful Thinking Patterns

These are patterns most people fall into from time to time. Recognising them is the first step:

- **All-or-nothing thinking**: Seeing things in black and white. "If I'm not perfect, I'm a failure."
- **Catastrophising**: Jumping to the worst possible outcome. "If I make a mistake, everything will fall apart."
- **Mind reading**: Assuming you know what others think. "They must think I'm incompetent."
- **Personalisation**: Blaming yourself for things outside your control. "It's my fault the team didn't do well."
- **Overgeneralisation**: Using one event to draw sweeping conclusions. "This always happens to me."
- **Mental filter**: Focusing only on the negatives while ignoring the positives.
- **Should statements**: Putting rigid pressure on yourself. "I should be able to handle this."

## How to Reframe a Thought

When you notice you are feeling upset, try these steps:

1. **Pause and name the feeling.** What emotion are you experiencing? Sadness, anxiety, frustration, shame?
2. **Identify the thought behind it.** What were you thinking just before the feeling intensified? Write it down if you can.
3. **Ask yourself some gentle questions:**
   - What evidence supports this thought? What evidence does not?
   - Am I confusing a feeling with a fact?
   - What would I say to a friend who had this same thought?
   - Is there another way to look at this situation?
4. **Try a more balanced thought.** This is not about forced positivity. It is about finding a perspective that is realistic and less distressing.

## Example

- **Situation**: You made a mistake at work.
- **Automatic thought**: "I'm terrible at my job. Everyone noticed."
- **Reframed thought**: "I made one mistake. Mistakes are normal. I have done good work before and I can learn from this."

## Important Notes

Reframing is a skill that takes practice. It is not about dismissing your feelings — your feelings are valid regardless. The goal is to check whether the thoughts fuelling those feelings are accurate and helpful.

If you find that unhelpful thoughts are persistent, overwhelming, or significantly affecting your daily life, working with a trained therapist can make a real difference. A professional can guide you through these techniques in a way that is tailored to your specific experiences.
