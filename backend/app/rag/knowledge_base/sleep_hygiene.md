# Sleep Hygiene Tips

Good sleep is foundational to mental and emotional wellbeing. When sleep is disrupted, everything else tends to feel harder — concentration, mood, patience, and the ability to cope with stress. The following are practical, evidence-informed strategies that many people find helpful.

## Building a Sleep-Friendly Routine

- **Consistent schedule**: Try to go to bed and wake up at roughly the same time each day, even on weekends. Your body clock benefits from regularity.
- **Wind-down period**: Give yourself 30 to 60 minutes before bed to shift into a calmer state. This could include light reading, gentle stretching, or listening to calm music.
- **Limit screens before bed**: The blue light from phones, laptops, and tablets can interfere with your body's natural sleep signals. If you must use a device, consider enabling a warm-light or night mode.
- **Avoid heavy meals late at night**: Eating a large meal close to bedtime can make it harder to fall asleep. A light snack is fine if you are hungry.
- **Limit caffeine after mid-afternoon**: Caffeine can stay in your system for 6 to 8 hours. Tea, coffee, and some soft drinks all contain caffeine.

## Creating a Comfortable Environment

- Keep your bedroom cool, dark, and quiet where possible.
- Use your bed primarily for sleep — try to avoid working, studying, or scrolling in bed so your brain associates the bed with rest.
- If noise is an issue, consider earplugs or a white noise app.

## When You Cannot Sleep

- If you have been lying awake for more than 20 minutes, get up and do something quiet and non-stimulating in dim light (such as reading a few pages of a book). Return to bed when you feel sleepy.
- Avoid clock-watching. Turn your clock or phone away from you to reduce the pressure of "I need to fall asleep now."
- Try a simple breathing exercise or body scan: slowly bring your attention to each part of your body from toes to head, noticing sensations without trying to change them.

## When to Seek Help

If sleep difficulties persist for several weeks, significantly affect your daytime functioning, or are accompanied by persistent low mood or anxiety, it is worth speaking to a healthcare professional. Sleep problems can sometimes be connected to other things that a professional can help you address.

*This is general wellbeing information, not clinical advice.*
