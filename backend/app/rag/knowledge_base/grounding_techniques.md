# Grounding Techniques

Grounding techniques help bring your attention back to the present moment when you feel overwhelmed, anxious, or disconnected. They work by redirecting your focus from distressing thoughts to your immediate surroundings and physical sensations.

## The 5-4-3-2-1 Technique

This sensory awareness exercise can help interrupt anxious or racing thoughts:

- Notice 5 things you can see. Look around and name them silently or aloud.
- Notice 4 things you can touch. Feel the texture of your clothes, the surface beneath you, the temperature of the air.
- Notice 3 things you can hear. Listen for sounds you might usually ignore — a clock ticking, distant traffic, birds.
- Notice 2 things you can smell. If you cannot detect anything, move to a place where you can, or recall a favourite scent.
- Notice 1 thing you can taste. Take a sip of water or simply notice the current taste in your mouth.

## Physical Grounding

- Hold an ice cube in your hand and focus on the cold sensation.
- Press your feet firmly into the floor and notice the support beneath you.
- Splash cold water on your face or run your hands under cold water.
- Clench and release your fists slowly, paying attention to the tension and release.

## Mental Grounding

- Count backwards from 100 by 7s.
- Pick a colour and look around the room naming everything you see in that colour.
- Describe an everyday activity in great detail (such as how you make tea or coffee), step by step.
- Recite something you know by heart — a poem, a song, or a list.

## When to Use Grounding

Grounding is most helpful when you notice early signs of anxiety, panic, dissociation, or emotional overwhelm. The earlier you start, the more effective it tends to be. There is no wrong way to do it — whatever brings your attention back to the present moment is working.

*This is general wellbeing information, not clinical advice. If you experience frequent or severe distress, please consult a qualified mental health professional.*
